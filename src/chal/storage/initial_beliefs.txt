# Thesis
- Stance: While metaphysical libertarian free will—choices uncaused by prior physical states—lacks empirical support due to neural precursors and causal closure (C1), a scientifically grounded form of free will consistent with compatibilism exists: humans engage in reason-responsive decision-making processes that align actions with goals and intentions (C2). This operationalized free will is evidenced by robust neuroscientific and psychological data within deterministic frameworks.
- Top bullets:
  - Libertarian free will is undermined by evidence of unconscious neural precursors to decisions (C1).
  - Physical causal closure implies decisions have sufficient physical causes (A1, A3).
  - Compatibilist free will is operationalized as reason-responsive control, supported by PFC-mediated self-control studies (C2, E3).
  - Quantum randomness does not confer agentic freedom (X1).
  - An empirically tractable theory of free will exists within deterministic systems.
- Strength: 0.52
- Reasoning: avg(0.70, 0.70) × (2^1.5 / (2^1.5 + 1)) = 0.70 × (2.828 / 3.828) = 0.70 × 0.739 = 0.5173 ≈ 0.52

# Scope & Definitions

# Assumptions
- [A1] (empirical) The physical world is causally closed: every physical event has a sufficient physical cause.
  - Strength: 0.85 (Widely accepted in physics; no confirmed violations of causal closure have been observed.)
  - Status: active
- [A2] (methodological) Compatibilist free will can be operationally defined as reason-responsive decision-making in accordance with one’s desires and intentions.
  - Strength: 0.75 (Standard in compatibilist literature though debates persist over precise criteria.)
  - Status: active
- [A3] (empirical) Neuroscientific methods (EEG, fMRI) can reliably detect neural precursors to decisions.
  - Strength: 0.8 (Multiple labs replicate predictive neural signatures prior to reported intentions.)
  - Status: active
- [A4] (methodological) Psychological experiments on self-control (e.g., Stroop tasks) validly measure deliberative, goal-directed processes.
  - Strength: 0.7 (Widely used paradigms with consistent behavioral and neural correlates, though interpretations vary.)
  - Status: active

# Evidence
- [E1] (empirical) Libet-style readiness potentials appear ~200ms before subjects report conscious intention.
  - Strength: 0.75 (Replicated in multiple studies but interpretation of RP remains debated.)
  - Source: Libet (1983) and replications
  - Supports: C1
  - Status: active
- [E2] (empirical) fMRI patterns predict simple choices up to 10 seconds before conscious awareness.
  - Strength: 0.7 (Robust effects though based on small sample sizes and simplified tasks.)
  - Source: Soon et al. (2008)
  - Supports: C1
  - Status: active
- [E3] (empirical) Prefrontal cortex activation correlates with successful inhibition and deliberative decision-making in Stroop and go/no-go tasks.
  - Strength: 0.8 (Converging evidence from fMRI, lesion and stimulation studies.)
  - Source: Neuropsychology literature
  - Supports: C2
  - Status: active

# Claims
- [C1] (descriptive) Empirical evidence shows decisions are initiated by unconscious neural processes prior to conscious awareness, implying that choices are causally determined.
  - Depends on: A1, A3, E1, E2
  - Inference chain:
    - Premise: The physical world is causally closed (A1).
    - Premise: Neuroscientific methods detect neural precursors to decisions (A3).
    - Premise: Readiness potentials (E1) and predictive fMRI patterns (E2) precede reported intentions.
    - Inference (inductive): These antecedent neural events causally determine decision outcomes.
    - Conclusion: Decisions are causally determined by prior neural states, undermining libertarian free will.
  - Strength: 0.7 (0.70 — supported by A1 (0.85), A3 (0.80), E1 (0.75), E2 (0.70); limited by E2 (0.70).)
  - Status: active
  - Predictions:
    - If truly uncaused decisions occur, some choices will show no detectable neural precursor.
      Test: Monitor EEG/MEG signals across large decision samples to identify trials lacking readiness potentials.
      Criterion: If >5% of decisions occur without any precursor signal, the claim is falsified.
      Falsifiers: Persistent undetectable neural signatures before decisions, Evidence of decision without precursor
- [C2] (descriptive) Humans exhibit compatibilist free will in that their deliberative reasoning processes respond to reasons and produce actions aligned with goals and intentions.
  - Depends on: A2, A4, E3
  - Inference chain:
    - Premise: Compatibilist free will is operationally defined as reason-responsive decision-making (A2).
    - Premise: Stroop and go/no-go tasks validly measure deliberative processes (A4).
    - Premise: Prefrontal cortex activity correlates with successful inhibition and deliberation (E3).
    - Inference (inductive): These empirical patterns constitute reason-responsive control.
    - Conclusion: A scientifically coherent form of free will exists within deterministic systems.
  - Strength: 0.7 (0.70 — supported by A2 (0.75), A4 (0.70), E3 (0.80); limited by A4 (0.70).)
  - Status: active
  - Predictions:
    - Enhancing prefrontal cortex function will improve reason-responsive decision-making.
      Test: Apply TMS to dorsolateral PFC and measure changes in Stroop/inhibition task performance.
      Criterion: If stimulation yields no significant improvement in deliberative control, the claim is challenged.
      Falsifiers: Null or negative effects of PFC modulation on deliberation

# Uncertainties
- [U1] (active) → targets: E1, E2, C1
  - Does the readiness potential conclusively indicate decision causation or merely correlate with preparatory processes?
  - Importance: high
- [U2] (active) → targets: C2, A2
  - To what extent does reason-responsive control equate to freedom in common-sense and moral responsibility?
  - Importance: medium

# Counterpositions
- [X1] (undermining) → targets: A1
  - Statement: Quantum indeterminacy introduces uncaused events, breaking causal closure.
  - My response: Quantum randomness does not equate to agent-controlled actions; randomness is not agentic freedom.
  - Sufficiency: sufficient
- [X2] (undercutting) → targets: C1
  - Statement: Readiness potentials may reflect general motor preparation, not specific decision initiation.
  - My response: Meta-analyses and multimodal studies refine signals to task-specific predictors, reinforcing their link to decision initiation.
  - Sufficiency: partial
- [X3] (rebutting) → targets: C2
  - Statement: Self-control experiments only show conditioned responses, not genuine normative agency.
  - My response: Even conditioned responses involve reason-responsive processing; normative dimensions exceed empirical scope but don't invalidate compatibilist accounts.
  - Sufficiency: partial

# Thesis
- Stance: A robust case can be made for libertarian free will: first-person phenomenology reveals genuine deliberation (C1), moral responsibility coherently presupposes the capacity to do otherwise (C2), and purely physical determinism lacks the resources to explain these phenomena, pointing to non-physical agency (C3).
- Top bullets:
  - Agents reliably report experiencing genuine deliberation and alternative possibilities (C1).
  - Moral responsibility requires genuine freedom of choice to ground praise and blame (C2).
  - Physical determinism and stochastic processes alone cannot account for the normative and phenomenological dimensions of free will (C3).
  - Converging conceptual and introspective evidence supports the reality of non-physical agency.
- Strength: 0.59
- Reasoning: avg(0.70, 0.70, 0.70) × (3^1.5 / (3^1.5 + 1)) = 0.70 × (5.196 / 6.196) = 0.70 × 0.839 = 0.587

# Scope & Definitions

# Assumptions
- [A1] (foundational) Free will is the capacity of an agent to choose among genuine alternative possibilities and to cause actions through autonomous decisions.
  - Strength: 0.9 (This is a standard libertarian definition of free will, widely used in philosophy.)
  - Status: active
- [A2] (empirical) First-person introspective reports of deliberation reliably indicate an agent's experience of making choices.
  - Strength: 0.7 (Introspection is fallible but generally accepted in psychology as evidence of mental states.)
  - Status: active
- [A3] (foundational) Moral responsibility presupposes that agents have the capacity to do otherwise.
  - Strength: 0.8 (Standard in moral philosophy: praise/blame require genuine alternative possibilities.)
  - Status: active

# Evidence
- [E1] (conceptual) Phenomenology of decision-making describes felt experiences of effort, deliberation, and alternative possibilities.
  - Strength: 0.8 (Widely reported in first-person accounts and recognized in phenomenological studies.)
  - Source: Philosophical phenomenology literature
  - Supports: C1
  - Status: active
- [E2] (expert_consensus) Surveys indicate around 58% of professional philosophers accept some form of libertarian free will or compatibilism involving genuine choice.
  - Strength: 0.5 (Reflects broad philosophical debate, though not a majority on strict libertarianism.)
  - Source: Pew Research and PhilPapers surveys
  - Supports: C1, C2, C3
  - Status: active
- [E3] (conceptual) Moral responsibility requires that actions are not fully determined by prior causes; otherwise, praise and blame would be unjustified.
  - Strength: 0.7 (Central premise in many theories of moral accountability.)
  - Source: Moral philosophy
  - Supports: C2
  - Status: active

# Claims
- [C1] (descriptive) Agents phenomenologically experience genuine deliberation and the ability to choose different courses of action.
  - Depends on: A1, A2, E1
  - Inference chain:
    - Premise: Free will is defined as capacity to choose among alternatives (A1).
    - Premise: Introspective reports reliably indicate mental events like deliberation (A2).
    - Premise: Phenomenology describes felt effort and choice (E1).
    - Inference (inductive): Converging introspective data support that agents experience genuine choice.
    - Conclusion: Agents phenomenologically experience genuine deliberation.
  - Strength: 0.7 (0.70 — supported by A1 (0.90), A2 (0.70), E1 (0.80); limited by A2 (0.70))
  - Status: active
  - Predictions:
    - If subjects under controlled decision-making tasks consistently report no sense of alternative possibilities, this claim is weakened.
      Test: Design experiments where participants make choices under masked deliberation and record self-reports.
      Criterion: If in >50% of trials participants deny any sense of choice, C1 is falsified.
      Falsifiers: Consistent absence of reported deliberation across many subjects
- [C2] (normative) Moral responsibility is only coherent if free will exists, because attributing praise or blame requires genuine alternative possibilities.
  - Depends on: C1, A3, E3
  - Inference chain:
    - Premise: Agents experience genuine choice (C1).
    - Premise: Moral responsibility presupposes the capacity to do otherwise (A3).
    - Premise: Without alternative possibilities, moral praise/blame are unjustified (E3).
    - Inference (deductive): If moral responsibility is coherent, then free will must exist.
    - Conclusion: Moral responsibility is only coherent if free will exists.
  - Strength: 0.7 (0.70 — supported by C1 (0.70), A3 (0.80), E3 (0.70); limited by C1/E3 (0.70))
  - Status: active
  - Predictions:
    - If moral systems coherently attribute responsibility under strict determinism, then this claim is falsified.
      Test: Identify or construct a moral theory that works without libertarian free will.
      Criterion: Existence of a widely accepted deterministic moral theory falsifies C2.
      Falsifiers: Robust compatibilist moral systems
- [C3] (descriptive) Phenomenological and moral arguments for free will cannot be accounted for by purely physical determinism, implying the existence of non-physical agency.
  - Depends on: C1, C2, A1
  - Inference chain:
    - Premise: Agents experience genuine choice (C1).
    - Premise: Moral responsibility requires free will (C2).
    - Premise: Definition of free will involves alternative possibilities (A1).
    - Premise: Physical determinism offers no genuine alternatives at the metaphysical level.
    - Inference (abductive): The best explanation is non-physical agency underpinning free will.
    - Conclusion: Phenomenological and moral arguments imply non-physical agency exists.
  - Strength: 0.7 (0.70 — supported by C1 (0.70), C2 (0.70), A1 (0.90); limited by C1/C2 (0.70))
  - Status: active
  - Predictions:
    - If advanced AI or purely physical systems exhibit indistinguishable genuine deliberation and moral responsibility, this inference is undermined.
      Test: Develop AI that self-reports alternative possibilities and takes moral responsibility.
      Criterion: If AI convincingly demonstrates both phenomena in a lab, C3 is falsified.
      Falsifiers: AI with truly autonomous choice and moral accountability
