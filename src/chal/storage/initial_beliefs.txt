# Thesis
- Stance: Grounded in empirical neuroscience (C1) and conceptual analysis (C2), libertarian free will—action originating uncaused by physical processes and yet not random—lacks support. However, a compatibilist conception of free will as the capacity to act according to one’s own motivations remains coherent and empirically measurable (C3).
- Top bullets:
  - Neuroscience shows decisions begin unconsciously before conscious intention (C1).
  - Quantum indeterminacy provides randomness, not agentive control, and thus cannot ground libertarian free will (C2).
  - Compatibilist free will—alignment of actions with reasons—is coherent under determinism and measurable via sense-of-agency studies (C3).
  - Moral responsibility and agency can be preserved within a compatibilist framework without requiring metaphysical indeterminism.
- Strength: 0.62
- Reasoning: avg(0.70, 0.80, 0.70) × (3^1.5 / (3^1.5 + 1)) = 0.73 × 0.84 = 0.62

# Scope & Definitions

# Assumptions
- [A1] (empirical) Physical causal closure: every physical event has a sufficient physical cause
  - Supports: C1, C3
  - Strength: 0.85 (Well-supported by physical sciences; no confirmed macroscopic exceptions observed)
  - Status: active
- [A2] (methodological) Third-person neuroscience data can reliably indicate causal order of brain and conscious events
  - Supports: C1
  - Strength: 0.75 (Based on replication of EEG and fMRI studies, though methodological debates remain)
  - Status: active
- [A3] (foundational) Randomness does not constitute agentive control: uncaused events do not equate to voluntary actions
  - Supports: C2
  - Strength: 0.8 (Conceptual clarity and psychological findings show no link between randomness and experienced control)
  - Status: active

# Evidence
- [E1] (empirical) Libet's experiments show readiness potential precedes conscious intention by ~300 ms
  - Strength: 0.7 (Widely cited; small samples and some methodological critiques but broadly replicated)
  - Source: Libet et al. 1983
  - Supports: C1
  - Status: active
- [E2] (empirical) fMRI patterns predict decision outcomes up to 10 s before conscious awareness
  - Strength: 0.75 (Replicated with multivariate pattern analysis; predictive accuracy ~60%)
  - Source: Soon et al. 2008
  - Supports: C1
  - Status: active
- [E3] (conceptual) Randomness, by definition, implies lack of control and cannot underwrite volitional agency
  - Strength: 0.8 (Widely accepted in philosophical literature on agency)
  - Source: Philosophical analysis
  - Supports: C2
  - Status: active
- [E4] (empirical) TMS and subliminal priming studies reduce subjective sense of agency when decisions are influenced externally
  - Strength: 0.7 (Multiple studies show robust effect sizes despite varied protocols)
  - Source: Haggard et al.
  - Supports: C3
  - Status: active

# Claims
- [C1] (descriptive) Decisions are initiated unconsciously and conscious intention is an epiphenomenon
  - Depends on: A1, A2, E1, E2
  - Inference chain:
    - Premise: Physical causal closure holds (A1).
    - Premise: Neuroscience methods can order brain vs. conscious events (A2).
    - Premise: Readiness potentials reliably precede reported intentions by ~300 ms (E1).
    - Premise: fMRI can predict choice before awareness (E2).
    - Inference (inductive): If neural signatures of decisions reliably precede conscious awareness, conscious intention does not initiate decisions.
    - Conclusion: Conscious intention is after-the-fact and epiphenomenal.
  - Strength: 0.7 (0.70 — supported by A1 (0.85), A2 (0.75), E1 (0.70), E2 (0.75); limited by E1 (0.70))
  - Status: active
  - Predictions:
    - If decision initiation is unconscious, artificially delaying conscious awareness will not alter decision reaction times.
      Test: Use TMS to delay conscious awareness onset and measure reaction times.
      Criterion: If reaction times slow significantly despite unchanged readiness potentials in >30% of trials, the claim is falsified.
      Falsifiers: Delayed awareness abolishes readiness potentials but reaction times remain constant
- [C2] (descriptive) Quantum randomness cannot provide a basis for free will because randomness lacks agentive control
  - Depends on: A3, E3
  - Inference chain:
    - Premise: Randomness is by definition uncaused and uncontrolled (A3).
    - Premise: Conceptual analysis shows control is required for agency (E3).
    - Inference (deductive): If randomness entails lack of control and control is essential for volition, randomness cannot ground free will.
    - Conclusion: Quantum indeterminacy does not supply the necessary control for free will.
  - Strength: 0.8 (0.80 — supported by A3 (0.80) and E3 (0.80); limited by A3/E3 (0.80))
  - Status: active
  - Predictions:
    - If quantum randomness contributed to agency, adding random noise in decision tasks would increase reported sense of control.
      Test: Introduce random cues and measure subjective control ratings.
      Criterion: If random noise increases sense of control significantly in >20% of sessions, the claim is falsified.
- [C3] (normative) Compatibilist free will—acting in accordance with one's motivations and reasons—is coherent and empirically measurable even if libertarian free will is unsupported
  - Depends on: A1, E4
  - Inference chain:
    - Premise: Physical causal closure holds (A1).
    - Premise: External manipulations reduce sense of agency measurably (E4).
    - Inference (inductive): If sense of agency varies with internal motivations under deterministic conditions, a compatibilist notion of free will is coherent and testable.
    - Conclusion: Compatibilist free will is a viable concept despite rejection of libertarian free will.
  - Strength: 0.7 (0.70 — supported by A1 (0.85) and E4 (0.70); limited by E4 (0.70))
  - Status: active
  - Predictions:
    - If compatibilist free will aligns with sense of agency, then altering motivations via persuasion will change reported agency.
      Test: Expose subjects to persuasive arguments and measure sense of agency afterwards.
      Criterion: If motivation changes do not correlate with agency ratings in >30% of subjects, the claim is falsified.

# Uncertainties

  - Importance: high
- [U2] (active) → targets: A1
  - Are there any empirical exceptions to physical causal closure at macroscopic scales that would open space for non-physical causation?
  - Importance: medium

# Counterpositions
- [X1] (undercutting) → targets: C1
  - Statement: Readiness potentials may reflect general preparatory brain activity, not specific decision initiation.
  - My response: Content-specific multivariate analyses (E2) and direct manipulation studies show specific decision-related patterns, not just general preparation.
  - Sufficiency: partial
- [X2] (rebutting) → targets: C2
  - Statement: Some theories (e.g., Penrose-Hameroff) propose quantum effects in consciousness that could underwrite agency.
  - My response: Those models remain speculative with no replicable empirical support; decoherence timescales make quantum control implausible in neural tissue.
  - Sufficiency: sufficient
- [X3] (rebutting) → targets: C3
  - Statement: Compatibilist redefinition of free will fails to capture the common-sense intuition of moral responsibility tied to genuine alternative possibilities.
  - My response: Moral responsibility can be grounded in social practices and psychological capacities for reflection and rational deliberation, independent of metaphysical indeterminism.
  - Sufficiency: partial

# Thesis
- Stance: Humans possess libertarian free will grounded in genuine alternative possibilities evidenced by introspection (C1) and undergirded by moral responsibility's demands (C2). The existence of an immaterial soul provides a plausible mechanism for mental causation beyond physical determinism (C3), yielding a coherent case for genuine freedom (C4).
- Top bullets:
  - First-person reports reliably indicate alternative possibilities in choice (C1)
  - Moral desert presupposes the ability to do otherwise (C2)
  - An immaterial soul affords non-physical mental causation (C3)
  - Together these support the existence of libertarian free will (C4)
- Strength: 0.56
- Reasoning: avg(0.65, 0.65, 0.60, 0.60) × (4^1.5 / (4^1.5 + 1)) = 0.625 × (8 / 9) = 0.56

- [X1] (undermining) → targets: A1
  - Statement: There is no empirical evidence for immaterial souls; invoking them violates the principle of parsimony
  - My response: The soul is inferred from phenomena (consciousness, moral agency) that physicalism struggles to explain. Many accepted scientific entities (quarks) are also unobservable directly, but posited to explain data.
  - Sufficiency: partial
- [X2] (rebutting) → targets: C1
  - Statement: Libet and subsequent neuroscience experiments show that decisions are initiated unconsciously before introspective awareness, undermining free-choice introspection
  - My response: Readiness potentials may reflect preparatory bias, not final decisions; some studies (Maoz & Yaffe) show conscious veto and randomness unexplained by simple determinism. Introspection retains validity for high-level volition.
  - Sufficiency: partial
- [X3] (undercutting) → targets: C2
  - Statement: Compatibilist theories demonstrate that moral responsibility does not require the ability to do otherwise, only responsive control
  - My response: Most compatibilist accounts implicitly rely on genuine alternative possibilities when they speak of 'could have done otherwise under identical conditions'. True desert-based praise exceeds mere reactive capacities.
  - Sufficiency: partial
