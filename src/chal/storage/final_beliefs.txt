# Thesis
- Stance: Our empiricist position holds that libertarian free will, requiring uncaused initiation and genuine alternative possibilities, lacks empirical support (C1, A1–A3, E1, E2, E5, E6). In contrast, agents exhibit compatibilist free will—choosing according to internal motivations and rational deliberation in the absence of coercion (C2, A4, E3)—and this framework suffices for moral responsibility (C3, A6, E7). Physicalism and deterministic neural findings ground a robust but non‐libertarian conception of autonomy while preserving practical responsibility. Key uncertainty remains whether neural precursors are strictly causative or merely correlative (U1).
- Top bullets:
  - Libertarian free will, demanding uncaused initiation, is unsupported by readiness potentials and veto studies (C1, E1, E2, E5, E6).
  - Compatibilist free will describes self‐determined choices arising from internal motivations and deliberation absent coercion (C2, A4, E3).
  - Compatibilist free will provides a sufficient basis for moral responsibility, as endorsed by expert consensus (C3, A6, E7).
  - Remaining high‐importance uncertainty: causal vs. correlative role of neural precursors (U1).
- Strength: 0.4325
- Reasoning: avg(0.40, 0.63, 0.70) × (3^1.0 / (3^1.0 + 1)) = 0.58 × 0.75 = 0.4325
