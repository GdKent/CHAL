# Thesis
- Stance: Under a compatibilist framework (D1, A1), converging behavioural, neural, and cognitive evidence indicates that humans exercise genuine volitional control.  Voluntary actions show distinct, causally validated neural signatures compared with coerced actions (C2).  Hierarchical predictive-processing demonstrates internal top-down causal influence (C4) while counterfactual reasoning guides adaptive choice (C5).  Newly added evidence that humans act on temporally projected reasons (C6) further underwrites long-horizon deliberative agency.  Remaining methodological uncertainties (U1, U2) warrant caution but do not outweigh the cumulative case.
- Top bullets:
  - Voluntary vs. coerced actions exhibit distinct causal neural patterns (C2).
  - Predictive-processing reveals internal top-down control mechanisms (C4).
  - Counterfactual reasoning drives adaptive decision updates (C5).
  - Temporal prospection links present choices to anticipated futures (C6).
- Strength: 0.544
- Reasoning: avg(0.47, 0.67, 0.80, 0.78) × (4^1.0 / (4^1.0 + 1)) = 0.68 × 0.80 = 0.544

# Definitions
- [D1] **free will**: The capacity of an agent to select among alternative courses of action through reason-responsive deliberation in the absence of external coercion, such that the agent would have acted differently had their reasons or deliberation differed.
  - Strength: 0.88 (0.80 — Widely used compatibilist formulation (Frankfurt, Fischer), broadly coherent yet contested by libertarian and hard-determinist definitions.)
  - Used by: A1, E1, E2, E3
  - Status: active
  - Defenses: 1 consecutive (original strength: 0.8)
- [D2] **external coercion**: Any force or influence that bypasses or overrides an agent’s deliberative processes (e.g., bodily restraint, direct neural stimulation) thereby determining the action independently of the agent’s reasoning.
  - Strength: 0.91 (0.85 — Aligned with legal and psychological literature; clearer boundary cases than the free-will concept itself.)
  - Used by: A1, E2
  - Status: active
- [D3] **reason-responsive**: A decision mechanism is reason-responsive when systematic variation in the reasons presented to an agent leads to systematic and predictable variation in the agent’s choices.
  - Strength: 0.82 (0.80 — Empirical operationalisation common in behavioural science although precise metrics vary.)
  - Used by: A2, E1
  - Status: active
- [D4] **predictive processing**: A neuro-computational framework in which the brain continuously generates and updates hierarchical predictions that guide perception and action
  - Strength: 0.87 (0.85 — Widely accepted in contemporary cognitive neuroscience; extensive empirical and computational support)
  - Used by: A3, E4
  - Status: active
- [D5] **counterfactual reasoning**: The cognitive capacity to mentally simulate alternative possible outcomes of an action or event and to use those simulations to guide subsequent decisions.
  - Strength: 0.86 (0.84 — Robust body of cognitive-science and neuroscience literature (e.g., Byrne 2016) confirms existence and functional role; minor open debates about developmental onset.)
  - Used by: A4, E5, E7
  - Status: active
- [D6] **temporal prospection**: The cognitive capacity to mentally project oneself into the future to evaluate long-term consequences of alternative actions.
  - Strength: 0.85 (0.85 — Extensive empirical and theoretical support in cognitive psychology and neuroscience (e.g., Schacter et al. 2017).)
  - Used by: A5, E8
  - Status: active

# Evidence
- [E1] (empirical) Behavioural experiments (e.g., Stroop, delay-discounting, moral dilemmas) show that when reasons are manipulated—such as providing new information or incentives—participants’ choices shift systematically in line with those reasons.
  - Strength: 0.8 (0.80 — Replicated across labs, large sample sizes, converging paradigms.)
  - Source: Meta-analysis of behavioural economics and cognitive control studies (Duckworth & Kern 2011; Hare et al. 2009).
  - Supports: C1
  - Supported by definitions: D1, D3
  - Status: active
- [E2] (empirical) fMRI and EEG studies reveal distinct neural signatures (greater pre-SMA and dorsolateral PFC engagement) for self-initiated actions versus actions performed under explicit coercion or direct stimulation.
  - Strength: 0.79 (0.75 — Moderate effect sizes, consistent across methodologies though some noise remains.)
  - Source: Haggard & Tsakiris 2009; Filevich et al. 2013.
  - Supports: C2
  - Supported by definitions: D1, D2
  - Status: active
  - Defenses: 1 consecutive (original strength: 0.75)
- [E3] (empirical) Re-analyses of Libet-style experiments show a subject’s ability to veto an impending movement up to ~200 ms before execution, and reveal that readiness potentials reflect stochastic fluctuation rather than committed decisions.
  - Strength: 0.0 (Retracted in concert with C3; no longer supports any active claim)
  - Source: Schurger et al. 2012; Schmidt et al. 2016.
  - Supports: C3
  - Supported by definitions: D1
  - Status: retracted
- [E4] (empirical) Laminar-specific recordings and TMS perturbations show that altering higher-level predictive signals changes voluntary movement choices, indicating causal top-down control
  - Strength: 0.77 (0.75 — Robust experimental paradigms with causal intervention; some methodological noise remains; limited by D4 (0.85))
  - Source: Auksztulewicz et al. 2022; Parr & Friston 2021 review
  - Supports: C4
  - Supported by definitions: D4
  - Status: active
- [E5] (empirical) Behavioural and fMRI studies (e.g., Coricelli et al. 2007; Daw et al. 2011) show that orbitofrontal cortex encodes counterfactual outcome values that predict subsequent choice adjustments in probabilistic tasks.
  - Strength: 0.8 (0.78 — Replicated across laboratories with model-based analyses; causal inference aided by lesion evidence; limited by D5 (0.84).)

  - Status: active

  - Strength: 0.76 (0.74 — Combines correlational and causal methods; preregistered replication; limited by D1 (0.86) & D2 (0.89).)

  - Strength: 0.78 (0.78 — Replicated across labs with convergent causal evidence (lesions/TMS); limited by D6 (0.85).)

# Claims
- [C1] (descriptive) Humans possess free will in the compatibilist sense because they routinely exhibit reason-responsive decision-making unimpeded by external coercion.
  - Depends on: A1, A2, E1, E7
  - Inference chain:
    - Premise (E1): Behavioural studies show systematic choice changes when reasons change (E1).
    - Premise (E7): Deterministic AI fails at deeper counterfactual tasks underlying such reason-responsiveness (E7).
    - Premise (A1): Reason-responsive behaviour without these deeper capacities is insufficient; humans possess the fuller suite under compatibilist scoping (A1).
    - Premise (A2): Behaviour and neural data are valid indicators of underlying psychological capacities (A2).
    - Inference (inductive): Given humans uniquely exhibit robust, counterfactual-sensitive reason-responsiveness, we inductively infer compatibilist free will.
    - Conclusion: Humans possess free will in the compatibilist sense because they routinely exhibit reason-responsive decision-making unimpeded by external coercion.
  - Strength: 0.0 (Retracted — evidential underdetermination and dependence on weakened scoping assumption A1 make the claim unsustainable at present.)
  - Status: retracted
  - Predictions:
    - Providing compelling new reasons (e.g., ethical arguments, monetary incentives) will alter choice distributions in statistically predictable ways.
      Test: Randomised controlled experiments varying the strength and content of reasons while measuring subsequent decisions.
      Criterion: If reason manipulations fail to change choices beyond a small effect size (d < 0.2) across multiple replications, this claim is weakened.
- [C2] (descriptive) Empirically distinguishable neural and behavioural patterns between voluntary and coerced actions support the reality of free versus unfree action categories.
  - Depends on: A1, A2, E2
  - Inference chain:
    - Premise (E2): Neuroimaging shows differential activation for self-initiated versus coerced movements.
    - Premise (A1): External coercion, by definition, negates free will.
    - Inference (inductive): If distinct neural/behavioural signatures reliably track coercion, then free and unfree actions are not illusory categories.
    - Conclusion: Empirically distinguishable neural and behavioural patterns between voluntary and coerced actions support the reality of free versus unfree action categories.
  - Strength: 0.47 (0.45 — lowered from 0.65 because its scoping dependency A1 was reduced to 0.45.  Evidence and causal-intervention arguments remain unchanged; claim is now capped by A1.)
  - Status: revised
  - Defenses: 1 consecutive (original strength: 0.75)
  - Predictions:
    - Under conditions experimentally classified as coercive, pre-SMA activation will be significantly reduced compared to voluntary conditions.
      Test: Within-subjects fMRI study comparing self-initiated actions, coaxed actions, and forced actions.
      Criterion: Failure to observe reliable activation differences (p > 0.05 across ROIs) would undermine this claim.
- [C3] (descriptive) Libet-style neuroscientific findings do not falsify compatibilist free will because the capacity for late veto and reason-responsive adjustment remains intact.
  - Depends on: A1, A2, E3
  - Inference chain:
    - Premise (E3): Participants retain an ability to cancel actions shortly before execution.
    - Premise (A1): Compatibilist free will requires reason-responsive control, not contra-causal initiation.
    - Inference (abductive): Because the empirical data demonstrate remaining control, the presence of early neural signals does not negate compatibilist free will.
    - Conclusion: Libet-style neuroscientific findings do not falsify compatibilist free will because the capacity for late veto and reason-responsive adjustment remains intact.
  - Strength: 0.0 (Retracted — Libet-style evidence remains too contested and was limiting thesis strength)
  - Status: retracted
  - Predictions:
    - If reasons to cancel an action are introduced during the readiness-potential window, cancellation rates will rise significantly.
      Test: Real-time neurofeedback study prompting moral or safety reasons shortly before movement execution.
      Criterion: If cancellation rates do not exceed baseline by at least 20%, this claim is weakened.
- [C4] (inductive) Hierarchical predictive processing demonstrates that internally generated reasons can causally influence human actions, supporting the existence of compatibilist free will
  - Depends on: A3, E4
  - Inference chain:
    - Premise (A3): Top-down predictive signals modulate action selection (A3)
    - Premise (E4): Causal-intervention studies confirm such signals influence movement choices (E4)
    - Inference (inductive): If internal predictive states causally steer action, then deliberative reasons can shape behaviour compatibly with free-will requirements
    - Conclusion: Hierarchical predictive processing demonstrates that internally generated reasons can causally influence human actions, supporting the existence of compatibilist free will
  - Strength: 0.67 (0.67 — reduced from 0.77 after critique established a hidden-premise gap: empirical evidence that predictive states are semantically ‘reasons’ remains limited. Strength now reflects this unresolved premise.)
  - Status: revised
  - Predictions:
    - Disrupting higher-level predictive regions (e.g., anterior cingulate) with TMS will systematically bias subsequent choice patterns
      Test: Within-subjects TMS study comparing choice distributions pre- and post-stimulation
      Criterion: If choice distributions remain unchanged (p > 0.05), claim is weakened
- [C5] (inductive) Human counterfactual reasoning causally shapes subsequent choices, demonstrating deliberative flexibility consistent with compatibilist free will.
  - Depends on: A4, E5
  - Inference chain:
    - Premise (A4): Humans engage in counterfactual reasoning that informs future behaviour (A4).
    - Premise (E5): Neuro-behavioural studies show counterfactual outcome signals predict subsequent choice changes (E5).
    - Inference (inductive): From the capacity and demonstrated causal impact of counterfactual reasoning, we inductively infer deliberative flexibility.
    - Conclusion: Human counterfactual reasoning causally shapes subsequent choices, demonstrating deliberative flexibility consistent with compatibilist free will.
  - Strength: 0.8 (0.78 — Supported by A4 (0.80) and E5 (0.78); limited by E5 (0.78).)
  - Status: active
  - Defenses: 1 consecutive (original strength: 0.78)
  - Predictions:
    - Disrupting orbitofrontal cortex activity with TMS will reduce counterfactual adjustment in choice behaviour.
      Test: Within-subject TMS study measuring change in behavioural adaptation to missed rewards.
      Criterion: If post-TMS counterfactual adjustment falls by <10 %, prediction is falsified.
- [C6] (inductive) Human temporal prospection enables selection of actions based on anticipated long-term consequences, evidencing compatibilist free will.
  - Depends on: A5, E8
  - Inference chain:
    - Premise (A5): Humans routinely engage in temporal prospection that incorporates future outcomes into present decisions.
    - Premise (E8): Neuroeconomic studies show future-value signals in PFC causally influence intertemporal choice.
    - Inference (inductive): If agents base actions on internally represented long-term reasons, they satisfy compatibilist criteria for free will.
    - Conclusion: Human temporal prospection enables selection of actions based on anticipated long-term consequences, evidencing compatibilist free will.
  - Strength: 0.78 (0.78 — Supported by A5 (0.80) and E8 (0.78); limited by lowest dependency E8 (0.78).)
  - Status: active
  - Predictions:
    - Disrupting PFC regions encoding future value (via TMS) will increase impulsive choices relative to baseline.
      Test: Within-subject TMS study measuring change in delay-discounting parameters.
      Criterion: If discounting rates rise by <10 % post-TMS, the prediction is falsified.

# Thesis
- Stance: Libertarian free will remains the best overall explanation of human agency.  Persistent gaps in neural prediction, even with state-of-the-art closed-loop BCIs (E8) together with reliable phenomenological testimony (A2, A3) underwrite the existence of real alternative possibilities (C1).  Deliberation exerts measurable top-down causal influence that cannot be reduced to prior micro-states (C4, supported by E10), and durable reason-responsive behaviour shifts surpass deterministic Bayesian models (C5, supported by E6, E9).  While cultural practices of moral responsibility (C3) are not decisive on their own, they coherently align with these data.  Collectively, the evidence indicates that human beings can genuinely choose otherwise, satisfying libertarian criteria for free will.
- Top bullets:
  - Neural prediction ceilings and conscious veto power leave room for genuine choice (C1).
  - High-resolution studies show deliberation triggers unpredictable yet reasoned choice reversals (C4).
  - Long-term persuasion effects demonstrate robust reason-responsive agency beyond deterministic updating (C5).
  - Universal moral-responsibility practices remain consistent with libertarian freedom (C3).

# Definitions
- [D1] **free will**: The genuine capacity of a conscious agent to choose between at least two courses of action such that, at the moment of choice, each alternative is metaphysically open to the agent.
  - Strength: 0.87 (0.85 — This definition is standard in libertarian free-will debates; while contested by compatibilists, it is precise and widely used in philosophical literature.)
  - Used by: A1, A2, A3, E1, E2
  - Status: active
  - Defenses: 1 consecutive (original strength: 0.85)
- [D2] **libertarian free will**: A stronger form of free will that is (a) incompatible with causal determinism and (b) entails agent causation not fully reducible to prior physical states.
  - Strength: 0.83 (0.83 — Standard usage among libertarian philosophers; precise though rejected by compatibilists.)
  - Used by: A4, E2
  - Status: active
- [D3] **moral responsibility**: The status of being legitimately praise- or blame-worthy for one’s actions in a way that justifies moral appraisal and potential sanction.
  - Strength: 0.82 (0.82 — Common cross-cultural and legal usage; boundary cases exist but the core is clear.)
  - Used by: A1, E1
  - Status: active
- [D4] **determinism**: The thesis that, given a complete specification of the universe at time t and the laws of nature, only one future is physically possible.
  - Strength: 0.8 (0.80 — Physics textbooks standard; challenged by quantum theory but concept itself is well defined.)
  - Used by: E2
  - Status: active
- [D5] **agent causation**: A causal relation where the originating source is an agent as a whole, not merely prior events or states within the agent.
  - Strength: 0.78 (0.78 — Philosophically coherent though controversial; concept has a clear lineage (Chisholm, O’Connor).)
  - Used by: E3
  - Status: active
- [D6] **quantum indeterminacy**: The ontological openness of outcomes described by quantum mechanics, such that certain events have probabilistic, not deterministic, distributions even with complete prior physical information.
  - Strength: 0.8 (0.80 — Supported by mainstream interpretations (Copenhagen, objective collapse) and empirical violations of Bell inequalities.)
  - Used by: A5
  - Status: active
- [D7] **rational deliberation**: The conscious process of evaluating reasons, weighing alternatives, and selecting an action in light of considered judgments.
  - Strength: 0.85 (0.85 — Standard concept in cognitive psychology and philosophy; empirically tractable.)
