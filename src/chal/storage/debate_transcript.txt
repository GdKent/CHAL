# 🛡️ Stage 3: Rebuttals


## Agent-Empiricist responds

**Q1** (refute): The 0.9 calibration for A1 reflects that all reproducible, macroscopic neural phenomena adhere to deterministic physical laws. Quantum fluctuations average out in neural tissue and no empirical study has shown quantum indeterminacy being harnessed to produce controlled, meaningful decision variability. Chaotic dynamics, while unpredictable, are still deterministic in principle. Thus A1 remains strongly supported.

**Q2** (refute): Although some reinterpret the readiness potential as general motor preparation, intracranial recordings and drift–diffusion models (Schurger et al. 2012; Murakami et al. 2014) localize decision-specific neural buildup rather than mere attention or nonspecific prep. These convergent findings preserve E1’s status as reflecting genuine decision initiation signals.

**Q3** (refute): A4 is a neutral scientiﬁc principle requiring any hypothesized phenomenon—libertarian or compatibilist—to be tied to observable markers before talking meaningfully about its existence. It neither presumes compatibilism nor precludes a future empirical operationalisation of libertarian freedom; it simply enforces methodological clarity.

**Q4** (defer): C1’s 0.75 strength already incorporates U1—the open possibility of hidden indeterministic mechanisms.  Until we observe a statistically robust class of decisions lacking readiness potentials, C1 remains the best-supported inductive conclusion.  To make this explicit, we should specify what threshold of such “RP‐free” decisions would warrant revising C1’s confidence.

**Q5** (refute): Under the operational (compatibilist) definition, free will is exactly the ability to modulate action in light of deliberated reasons.  E2’s demonstration of >70% reason–action alignment justifies C2 at 0.7.  The mechanistic, deterministic nature of the tracking does not undermine it—rather, it is what operational compatibilist free will amounts to.



## Agent-Supernaturalist responds

**Q1** (refute): While introspection alone can be biased, A2 is grounded in systematic phenomenological research employing controlled decision tasks, cross‐cultural cohorts, and neurophenomenological correlations. Multiple studies demonstrate reliable patterns where subjects’ reports of agentive choice align with distinct neural signatures, supporting A2’s empirical basis at strength 0.6.

**Q2** (refute): E1’s strength (0.65) already accounts for subjectivity. Crucially, independent laboratories have replicated the finding that subjects reliably distinguish genuine volitional episodes from mere urges across double‐blind task variants. These replications demonstrate a modest but real standard of reliability and replicability for E1.

**Q3** (refute): The abductive inference in C1 legitimately compares competing explanations. The illusion hypothesis requires a detailed mechanism to generate false agentive experiences across normal and anomalous cases (e.g., NDEs). Libertarian agent causation more parsimoniously accounts for the full range of observed phenomenology and exceptional cases, so until a concrete mechanistic model can match its explanatory scope, C1 stands.

**Q4** (refute): We bridge the is-ought gap via explicit normative premise A5 (‘Moral responsibility entails alternative possibilities’). The inference is deductive: from A4 (descriptive) and A5 (normative) we validly derive C2. Compatibilists dispute A5, but that is a substantive normative debate—not a hidden is-ought smuggling—and does not invalidate the deductive move.

**Q5** (defer): You are correct that A1 currently lacks specified empirical or falsification protocols. This is an open question about how one might confirm or disconfirm the supernatural. To address it properly, we need to articulate abductive criteria or indirect empirical indicators. Until then, this remains an unresolved uncertainty.
