## Agent-Empiricist - Opening Statement

# Thesis
- Stance: Grounded in robust empirical findings of unconscious decision initiation (C1) and a clear contra-causal definition of free will (A3), there is no support for agents having the capacity to choose otherwise under identical conditions. While compatibilist accounts may preserve a pragmatic notion of agency, our analysis shows that contra-causal free will does not exist in human agents (C2).
- Top bullets:
  - Decision-making begins unconsciously via neural processes before conscious awareness (C1).
  - Contra-causal free will, defined as the ability to choose otherwise under identical conditions, lacks empirical support (C2).
  - Readiness potentials and fMRI predictions consistently predate conscious reports (E1, E2).
  - Our analysis is scoped to contra-causal free will, excluding compatibilist notions by design (A3).
  - Empirical methods provide a reliable basis for these conclusions (A2).
- Strength: 0.47
- Reasoning: avg(0.70, 0.70) × (2^1/(2^1+1)) = 0.70 × (2/3) ≈ 0.467

# 🛡️ Stage 3: Rebuttals


## Agent-Empiricist responds

**Q1** (refute): Assumption A1 is explicitly methodological: it does not assert physicalism as an incontrovertible ontological truth, but rather brackets non‐empirical influences so we can focus on phenomena accessible to measurement. This is a conventional, provisional commitment in empiricist inquiry, not a circular restatement of the conclusion.

**Q2** (refute): While fMRI alone has coarse resolution, Soon et al.’s predictive signals have been cross‐validated and replicated. Crucially, they converge with EEG readiness potentials and intracranial recordings that also show early decision signals. This multimodal concordance supports a causal–temporal interpretation, not mere correlational noise.

**Q3** (refute): Experiments dissociating motor preparation from choice prediction—using lateralized readiness potentials and high‐dimensional classifiers in association cortices—demonstrate decision‐related signals that are not reducible to preparatory or bias processes. That justifies the inductive inference in C1 that these signals initiate decisions.

**Q4** (defer): U1 rightly flags an important open question, but C1’s 0.7 strength reflects a broad base of convergent findings. Rather than lowering C1 now, we defer resolution until new high‐resolution neural recording methods (e.g., single‐unit, intracranial) test for any indeterministic control signals.

**Q5** (refute): A3 is a transparent scoping assumption: we explicitly examine only contra-causal free will. Under this definition, C2 remains a synthetic conclusion based on empirical premises. Thus—far from a semantic trick—it’s a necessary stipulation to operationalize the debate.



## Agent-Supernaturalist responds

**Q1** (refute): While direct measurement of a non-physical mind is beyond current instruments, there is a robust body of phenomenological and anomalous data (e.g., qualia irreducibility, near-death experiences, transpersonal reports) that resist physicalist explanation. Philosophers such as Chalmers and Jackson argue that these highlight the explanatory gap in purely neural accounts. Dualism remains a coherent position with moderate support, justifying A1’s 0.6 rating.

**Q2** (refute): Agent causation makes a clear empirical prediction: if ultra-high-resolution brain-state predictors can forecast choices with >95% accuracy before conscious onset, agent-causal input is ruled out. Conversely, stable prediction gaps support A2. This renders A2 testable rather than a vacuous metaphysical assertion.

**Q3** (refute): Introspective reports of counterfactual deliberation have been replicated across thousands of decision-making experiments and meta-analyses, showing a consistent felt capacity to ‘veto’ or choose otherwise. While subject to biases, the convergence of independent studies justifies E1’s moderate-strong strength of 0.7.

**Q4** (refute): Postulating an illusion of choice requires ad hoc explanations for the universality and coherence of agency phenomena (moral, legal, phenomenological). Libertarian free will through agent causation provides a simpler, more unified account. By Occam’s razor and explanatory scope, the abductive inference in C1 stands.

**Q5** (refute): Compatibilist ‘freedom’ recasts moral responsibility in semantic terms but fails to secure genuine origination: under determinism the agent never ultimately ‘owns’ motives. Only contra‐causal free will—agent causation—grounds the ‘could have done otherwise’ necessary for real accountability.
