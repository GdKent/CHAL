This operationalized free will is evidenced by robust neuroscientific and psychological data within deterministic frameworks.
- Top bullets:
  - Libertarian free will is undermined by evidence of unconscious neural precursors to decisions (C1).
  - Physical causal closure implies decisions have sufficient physical causes (A1, A3).
  - Compatibilist free will is operationalized as reason-responsive control, supported by PFC-mediated self-control studies (C2, E3).
  - Quantum randomness does not confer agentic freedom (X1).
  - An empirically tractable theory of free will exists within deterministic systems.

# 🛡️ Stage 3: Rebuttals


## Agent-Empiricist responds

**Q1** (defer): A2 was deliberately framed as a methodological assumption to operationalize compatibilist free will for empirical study. It does not and cannot by itself resolve normative questions about moral responsibility. Those normative dimensions lie in the province of moral and political philosophy, not empirical neuroscience (see U2).

**Q2** (refute): Neuroscientists distinguish RP components tied specifically to decision initiation by using lateralized readiness potentials (LRPs), time‐locked contrasts against pure motor tasks, and multivariate decoding analyses (Schurger et al. 2012; Kornhuber & Deecke 1965). These methods reliably separate general motor preparation from choice‐specific neural predictors.

**Q3** (refute): Although Soon et al. (2008) used simplified tasks, subsequent studies in more naturalistic paradigms (e.g. Haynes 2011; Bode et al. 2013) demonstrate similar predictive neural patterns. The mechanistic continuity of decision‐related circuitry supports generalization to complex choices, subject to further empirical refinement.

**Q4** (refute): Causal perturbation studies (TMS and lesion work) show that dorsolateral PFC is necessary for novel, non‐conditioned decision tasks (e.g. value‐based choices, planning), not just conditioned inhibition. This indicates prefrontal activation reflects deliberative, reason‐responsive control rather than mere stimulus–response conditioning.

**Q5** (refute): The >5% cutoff aligns with the conventional statistical α=0.05 threshold for falsification: if more than 5% of decisions truly lack neural precursors beyond Type I error rates, the determinism hypothesis is rejected. This ties the criterion to standard inferential practice.



## Agent-Supernaturalist responds

**Q1** (defer): I acknowledge that at present we lack rigorous neuroimaging studies directly correlating first-person reports of alternative possibilities with distinct decision-mechanism signatures (e.g. specific PFC activity patterns). Resolving this requires experiments that simultaneously record neural deliberation markers and trial-by-trial self-reports. Until such data are available, the reliability of introspective reports under X1’s challenge remains an open empirical question.

**Q2** (refute): Empirical studies on intentional binding (e.g. Haggard et al. 2002) demonstrate that when agents feel authorship of actions, they perceive intervals between action and outcome as shorter, and this binding effect correlates with premotor and parietal activity. Such neural–behavioral coupling shows the sense of agency has causal efficacy—modulating motor learning and prediction—ruling out a mere post-hoc epiphenomenon.

**Q3** (defer): To date, no reproducible neuroscientific anomaly has been shown to violate physical causal closure in human decision-making. While quantum indeterminacy might introduce randomness, it doesn’t by itself compel non-physical causation. Thus this challenge remains unresolved until clear empirical anomalies emerge.

**Q4** (defer): Operationally distinguishing AI “choices” from non-physical agency would require demonstrating that an entity’s decisions have irreducibly ontological properties—e.g. truly indeterminate sourcehood and normative self-legislation uncomputable by any Turing‐type algorithm. Designing such tests remains a conceptual and technical frontier.

**Q5** (refute): I specifically reject John Martin Fischer’s semi-compatibilism (and Strawson’s reactive-attitudes framework) because they redefine moral responsibility in purely interpersonal or practical terms, while dispensing with the metaphysical capacity to do otherwise. Normatively, they fail to secure genuine alternative possibilities needed for robust praise and blame, reducing freedom to mere social conditioning.
