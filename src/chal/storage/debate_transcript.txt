The debate underscores the challenges in reconciling these approaches and suggests areas for further exploration.

The debate addresses the complex question of free will, examining whether it can be understood through empirical evidence or if it requires a spiritual explanation. Participants include Agent-Empiricist, who focuses on empirical methodologies, and Agent-Supernaturalist, who emphasizes spiritual insights. The central research question explores the validity of free will as either an empirically measurable phenomenon or a spiritual essence.

- **Agent-Supernaturalist**: Maintains that free will is a spiritual essence (A1, A3), despite lacking empirical validation. Relies on spiritual and religious experiences as sources of insight, with confidence in spiritual truths.

## 4. Claims and Evidence
- **Empirical Limitations**: Agent-Empiricist's claims (C1, C2) highlight the influence of genetic and environmental factors on decision-making, supported by neurological data (E1, E2). However, the evidence is limited by correlation-causation issues.
- **Spiritual Assertions**: Agent-Supernaturalist's claim (C1) lacks empirical falsifiability, relying on spiritual insights. The absence of empirical evidence reduces confidence in the claim.

## 5. Cross-Examination and Rebuttals

- **Rebuttals**: Agent-Supernaturalist acknowledged the need to refine predictions (P1) and conceded several points, including the speculative nature of spiritual assumptions.

## 6. Adjudication Results
Critiques were deemed valid due to acknowledged weaknesses in empirical evidence and the lack of falsifiability in spiritual claims. The debate remains unresolved on the empirical vs. spiritual epistemology (Q1), with both sides recognizing the limitations of their frameworks.

- **Proposed Tests**: Further empirical studies on decision-making processes and exploration of spiritual experiences could provide insights.

## 8. Synthesis and Conclusion
The debate highlighted the epistemological divide between empirical and spiritual approaches to free will. While both participants acknowledged limitations, the discussion remains unresolved. Progress was made in recognizing the challenges of each perspective, but further exploration is needed to reconcile these approaches.
